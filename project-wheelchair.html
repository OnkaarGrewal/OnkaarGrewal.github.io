Overview

The ENGINEER 1P13 capstone challenged our team to design an assistive device for a real client — a wheelchair user with arthritis who wanted to play ball sports independently. Our solution was a wheelchair-mounted launcher capable of throwing a ball 6+ metres with adjustable direction and force.

I led the mechanical CAD design, modelling the full assembly in SolidWorks and using Granta EduPack to select mounting hardware materials that could handle repeated launch loads without fatiguing the wheelchair frame.

The project required coordinating across sub-teams handling electronics, user interface, and structural testing — the first time I managed technical design integration across a full multidisciplinary group.

Specifications

What I Learned

◆
Material selection under load Using Granta EduPack to cross-reference yield strength, density, and corrosion resistance under real constraints — not just picking "aluminium" because it's familiar.
◆
Design for the actual user Accessibility constraints forced decisions I wouldn't have made otherwise — tool-free mounting, one-handed operation. Constraints produce better engineering.
◆
Integrating across sub-teams Coordinating CAD with the electronics team's component footprints required discipline around version control — first time I used SolidWorks in a shared-model context.

Aerospace Connection

Granta EduPack material selection is standard practice in aerospace structural design. The same process I used here — defining a performance index, filtering by constraints, and ranking candidates — is how engineers select materials for airframe brackets, satellite bus panels, and launch vehicle skins.

The 50 N launch load analysis also introduced me to how fatigue and cyclic loading are accounted for in design — a core concept in any aerospace structure that sees repeated stress cycles in service.
