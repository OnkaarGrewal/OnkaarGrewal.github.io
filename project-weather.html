Overview

This started as a straightforward sensor project and grew into a proper engineering problem once I started thinking about the housing. Getting a sensor to read correctly inside a plastic box exposed to rain, humidity, and temperature swings is harder than it sounds.

The system measures temperature, humidity, wind speed, and rainfall using a sensor suite communicating over I²C and SPI. Data is logged to an SD card in real time, with a timestamp from an RTC module.

The housing was designed in SolidWorks with deliberate attention to ventilation — the temperature and humidity sensors need airflow, but the electronics need to stay dry. I went through several housing iterations before the current design.

Housing Design Process

The first housing prototype failed immediately — moisture got into the sensor cavity and the DHT22 readings drifted by 15% within an hour. The fix was adding a labyrinthine ventilation channel that lets air circulate without letting water in.

The second challenge was thermal management. The Arduino generates heat, and if that heat is trapped near the temperature sensor, you get a systematic reading error. I added a thermal baffle separating the microcontroller cavity from the sensor cavity.

What I Learned

Sensor enclosure designVentilation and thermal isolation are not afterthoughts — they determine whether your sensor reads the environment or the inside of your box.
◆
Embedded C/C++ disciplineMemory is tight on ATmega328P. Writing lean, interrupt-driven code for sensor polling forced me to think carefully about data types and timing.
◆
